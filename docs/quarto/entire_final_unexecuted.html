Introduction

Improving yield, seed protein, and oil content remain a central challenge in soybean breeding because these traits are economically critical yet genetically complex. Yield is highly polygenic and strongly influenced by environmental variation @Sharma2024, while protein and oil content often exhibit antagonistic genetic correlations @Diers2018, making simultaneous improvement difficult. Breeders must also contend with genotype × environment interactions (G×E), which cause genotypes to perform inconsistently across locations and years, reducing the reliability of selection decisions @Akram2016. Traditional phenotypic selection is further constrained by the cost and time required to generate multi-environment field data, as well as the limited ability to dissect the underlying genetic architecture of these traits.

Although phenotypic selection alone is time consuming and not maximally effective, accurate phenotyping is crucial for understanding the heritability and variance components of traits and for obtaining accurate results from genomic inferences and predictions. Heritability refers to the ability of a trait to be passed down from parent to progeny or the portion of total phenotypic variance that can be attributed to genotype. An accurate understanding of heritability is crucial for estimating a population’s response to selection and the potential genetic gain that can be expected @Holland2003. A higher heritability suggests that selection is simpler, and the trait is likely only controlled by one or a few genes, while low heritability is typically associated with complex, polygenic traits. The variance components involved in phenotypic variance and heritability, such as dominance and additive variance, can further be parsed apart, and linear mixed models are typically used for this.

Advances in high-density SNP genotyping (e.g., SoySNP50K) and mixed model statistical frameworks now enable more precise estimation of genetic effects, partitioning of G×E, and prediction of breeding values using genome-wide markers (Ravelombola et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2016). The genomic data acquired by SNP genotyping can be used for various downstream applications that allow breeders to make both large-scale inferences and powerful predictions. For example, SNP data and phenotypic data can be collected from a diverse population and analyzed in a genome-wide association study (GWAS), which allows researchers to identify SNPs in regions of the genome that are significantly associated with a trait of interest @Clauw2025. Genotypic and phenotypic data can also be used for genomic selection (GS), which involves building and training a model to predict breeding values of individuals based on a score at each SNP @Escamilla2025.

By integrating multi-environment phenotypic data with genomic information, this project aims to characterize the genetic architecture of yield, protein, and oil content, quantify their heritability, and evaluate the potential of genomic prediction to accelerate selection for improved seed quality and productivity in soybean. We will infer variance components and heritability using phenotypic data, identify SNPs associated with oil, protein, and yield using GWAS, and predict the performance of progeny from a subset of lines in this data using a genomic selection (GS) model.

Data sources and type

Genotypic data. Imputed genotypes of the 5,176 Nested Association Mapping (NAM) recombinant inbred lines (RILs), derived from 40 NAM parental genotypes and obtained using the SoySNP50K and SoyNAM6K assays, were used in this project. The SNP arrays were aligned to the Williams 82 reference genome assembly (Wm82.a2). The imputed genotype dataset is publicly available at SoyBase.

Phenotypic and metadata. Phenotypic data and associated metadata were obtained from the USDA Soybean Nested Association Mapping (SoyNAM) project, which provides large‑scale field evaluations of soybean lines across multiple locations and years to study complex agronomic and seed composition traits. The SoyNAM project resources are available at SoyBase Legacy.

Variance decomposition and heritability

Variance components were estimated for yield, protein, and oil using linear mixed models with random effects for genotype, environment, and genotype‑by‑environment interaction (G×E). For each trait, the fitted model partitioned the total phenotypic variance into contributions from these components and the residual error. Proportional variance contributions were computed to compare the relative influence of genotype, environment, and G×E across traits. Broad‑sense heritability across environments was then estimated using variance components adjusted for the number of environments and total observations contributing to each trait.

# Proportion of variance for any trait
get_varprop <- function(trait_name) {
  
  dat <- pheno_raw[!is.na(pheno_raw[[trait_name]]), ]
  
  form <- as.formula(
    paste0("`", trait_name, "` ~ (1|`Corrected Strain`) + (1|Env) + (1|`Corrected Strain`:Env)")
  )
  
  mod <- lmer(form, data = dat)
  
  vc <- as.data.frame(VarCorr(mod))[, c("grp", "vcov")]
  
  vc$grp[vc$grp == "Corrected Strain"] <- "Genotype"
  vc$grp[vc$grp == "Env"] <- "Environment"
  vc$grp[vc$grp == "Corrected Strain:Env"] <- "GxE"
  vc$grp[vc$grp == "Residual"] <- "Residual"
  
  vc$Proportion <- vc$vcov / sum(vc$vcov)
  vc$Trait <- trait_name
  
  vc[, c("Trait", "grp", "vcov", "Proportion")]
}

# Broad-sense heritability across environments
compute_H2 <- function(trait_name) {
  
  dat <- pheno[!is.na(pheno[[trait_name]]), ]
  
  n_env <- length(unique(dat$Env))
  n_obs <- nrow(dat)
  
  form <- as.formula(
    paste0(trait_name, " ~ (1|Strain) + (1|Env) + (1|Strain:Env)")
  )
  
  mod <- lmer(form, data = dat)
  
  vc <- as.data.frame(VarCorr(mod))
  
  sigma_g  <- vc$vcov[vc$grp == "Strain"]
  sigma_ge <- vc$vcov[vc$grp == "Strain:Env"]
  sigma_e  <- vc$vcov[vc$grp == "Residual"]
  
  H2 <- sigma_g / (sigma_g + (sigma_ge / n_env) + (sigma_e / n_obs))
  
  list(
    Trait = trait_name,
    H2 = H2,
    sigma_g = sigma_g,
    sigma_ge = sigma_ge,
    sigma_e = sigma_e,
    n_env = n_env,
    n_obs = n_obs
  )
}

GWAS

GWAS Background

A genome wide association study (GWAS) is a powerful tool which allows researchers to identify SNPs and genomic regions associated with traits of interest in a population. GWAS requires a diverse population, high quality genomic data, and accurate phenotypic data to render useful results. The discovery of associated SNPs relies on the high density of polymorphic markers the genomes of a population and the linkage disequilibrium that causes certain SNPs to remain near casual polymorphisms during recombination.

The basic statistical principle of a GWAS is fairly simple; however, the huge number of SNPs that must be tested and the limited amount of phenotypic data relative to genotypic data make GWAS more complicated and computing intensive. Another complicating factor is that genetic relatedness within the test population can lead to false positive associations at SNPs (He & Gai, 2023). This is accounted fro using a kinship matrix and multiple statistical models. The basic GWAS formula is the foundation for all analyses ad is shown below.

\[ y = µ+xβ + ε \] Where y is the phenotype, x is the genotype at each SNP coded as 0, 1, or 2 µ is the mean value of individuals, β is the effect of the SNP, and ε is the residual error.

Due to the high importance of yield, oil, and protein in soybean cultivars, the identification of novel SNPs that are associated with these traits is extremely valuable for breeders when making selections. The goal of this part of the project was to run multiple GWAS analyses using several different models for each of the three traits mentioned. Expected vs actual log p-values were used to determine which models limited p-value inflation, and these models were selected. SNPs that were found to be significant across multiple models are likely to be good candidates for future research and integration into MAS or GS. Once fully validated, these SNPs can be used during selection of early generation crosses within the tested population to cull inferior plants and make greater genetic gain.

## hand calculate kinship matrix
kinship<-function(M){
  freq <- colMeans(M, na.rm = T)/2
  P <- matrix(rep(freq, nrow(M)), byrow = T, ncol = ncol(M))
  Z <- M - 2 * P
  varD <- sum(2 * freq * (1 - freq))
  K <- tcrossprod(Z)/varD
  return(K)
}

Genomic Prediction and Selection

Genomic Selection Background

Genomic prediciton and selection (GS) rely on a variety of statistical approaches to build models and predict phenotypic data for individuals using genotypic data from a training population. The prediction model based on the training population must be generalizeable to later generations in that population to be useful, so multiple models and methods have been adapted to reduce overfitting and accomplish this goal. In brief, n-fold cross validation is performed when the entire population is split into two random groups containing different individuals from the population. One is smaller and contains the test individuals, while the larger group contains the individuals that the model is trained on. The model built on the larger data set is then used to predict the value of the test data set. The predicted values are then compared to the actual values, and the correlation between predicted and observed data produces the prediction accuracy of that model.

Bayesian models have also been used to create test and train populations and determine prediction accuracy. In this part of the project, we create 10-fold G-BLUP cross validation models, genetic relationship matrices, and Bayesian models and determine the ability of these models to predict accuracy for yield, oil, and protein. The models are supplied with phenotypic and genotypic data, and the entire population is broken into a training (80% of data) and test (20% of data) group that is consistently used for all models. GEBVs from the top 10 lines for each trait were extracted from the best performing model, and superior lines will be used in future breeding.

##GS Results Sumary

The models used for genomic prediction of oil and protein had a significantly higher prediction accuracy than those for yield. For oil, BayesB had the highest accuracy out of the four models tested which was 0.684. For protein, Bayes Lasso was the best prediction model with an accuracy of 0.651. The high prediction accuracy obtained by these models suggests that they can be incorporated into a breeding pipeline to make selections based on genotypic markers and predicted GEBVs.This could help breeders make genetic gain more quickly and could help select individual that have high GEBVs for both oil and yield. Selection and breeding of those individuals could help reduce the negative correlation between oil and protein levels in soybean.

The models predicting soybean yield had the lowest prediction accuracy out of the three traits analyzed. The BayesB model produced the highest prediction accuracy for yield out of the four models tested, which was 0.384. This level of accuracy is probably not quite high enough to incorporate into a breeding pipeline; however, increasing the amount of genotypic data for each line via 50k SNP chips could help improve the prediction accuracy to a more useful level.

The lower prediction accuracy for yield is expected because the heritability for yield was lower than oil and protein, and environmental factors typically have a large impact on yield. This means that prediction based solely on genotypic data and GEBVs should be lower for yield.
